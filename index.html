Sikh-Mughal Clashes for Power

Battles of Valor: Epic Struggles in Sikh-Mughal Power Dynamics

Backstory

Birth of Sikhism

The South Asian region of Punjab, which is now part of the contemporary nations of India and Pakistan, is where Sikhism originated. Islam and Hinduism were the two most prevalent religions in the region at the time. Around 1500 CE, Guru Nanak started teaching a religion that he believed was different from both Islam and Hinduism. This is when the Sikh faith first emerged. Though he also brought many new concepts from his own philosophy, Nanak's religious ideas also drew from Islamic and Hindu theology. Monotheistic Sikhism holds that there is only one God, devoid of all forms, and that all people are created equal in God's eyes. Sikhs hold that karma—one's good and bad deeds—affects the quality of one's subsequent life and that reincarnation is an ongoing process of life, death, and rebirth. Additionally, Sikhism places more value on sincere deeds than on the ritualistic performance of religious rites.

Although there are many stories in Sikh tradition regarding Guru Nanak's life, very little has been learned about his actual existence. According to Sikh beliefs, he was devoted to God from a young age. Although his parents were Hindus, Nanak quickly showed a sophisticated interest in religion, studied Islam and Hinduism in depth, and showed remarkable aptitude as a poet and philosopher. In accordance with Sikh beliefs, Nanak undertook thirty-year spiritual excursions as an adult, traveling through India, Tibet, and Arabia. During these trips, he conversed and studied with the wise men he encountered. He started teaching a new path to enlightenment and a fulfilling existence as his thoughts took shape.

Following Nanak, the Sikh faith and society were established during the following century by nine Gurus, or spiritual instructors. By appointing the Sikh text, the Guru Granth Sahib, as his successor, the tenth guru, Gobind Singh, ended the succession of human gurus and established the scripture as the eleventh and final everlasting guru, a spiritual and religious guide for Sikhs. Within the Sikh tradition, the scripture known as the Guru Granth Sahib is the final authority. The musical compositions of the Gurus are included in this book, which was assembled by them. The works of other South Asian spiritual leaders with a similar philosophy were also adopted by the Gurus. The nature of heavenly experience and the means by which it can be attained are central themes in the compositions found in scripture. The Guru Granth Sahib is a key component in Sikh ceremonial and devotional life, as they view it as a revealed text.

A thorough counterexample to the Guru Granth Sahib's authority may be found in the Nirankari sect, who believe that their guru is the true and ultimate authority. They do not regard the teachings of the Guru Granth Sahib as the final word and instead adhere to their own holy text. This contradicts the widely held belief that the Guru Granth Sahib is the final authority in Sikhism. One could argue that the Guru Granth Sahib is not the final authority if it contains writings by other spiritual masters with similar views, implying that there are other sources of spiritual instruction besides this scripture.

The Mughal Influence

As the Sikh faith emerged and continued to evolve, the Mughal Empire came to prominence in India during the lifetime of Guru Nanak. As a result, the Mughals had a major influence on the establishment of Sikhism.

Early Mughal emperors were generally accepting of other religious traditions, including Emperor Akbar. Akbar's inclusive style of government was demonstrated in 1567 during his trip to Punjab and his presence at a communal Sikh feast known as langur. The Sikhs and the Mughals coexisted rather peacefully throughout this time.

The Sikhs, however, were viewed as a threat by the Mughal Emperor Jahangir since they supported a likely failed heir. In addition to trying to change society, he was accepting of Jews, Christians, and Hindus. However, there was tension with the Sikhs. The Sikh province was considered 'a state within a state' and was therefore almost self-governing, which was the cause of this dissatisfaction. He made demands in 1606 for changes to the Sikh holy book from Guru Arjan Dev, the fifth Guru of Sikhism. Since Guru Arjan Dev would not budge on the Sikh texts, Jahangir had him executed. This incident prompted the Sikhs to militarize in order to defend their faith and signaled the start of more serious hostilities between them and the Mughal Empire.

Following that, the Sikhs coexisted peacefully with the Mughal emperors until the reign of Emperor Aurangzeb, who attempted to impose Islamic ideology and religious uniformity on the people despite the Quran's prohibition against forced conversions. He ordered the arrest and execution of Guru Tegh Bahadur, the ninth Sikh Guru, in 1675 because he refused to convert to Islam. The martyrdom of Guru Tegh Bahadur represented his dedication to defending everyone's right to practice their own religion as well as the Sikh faith. Sikhs were persecuted more severely after his execution, which encouraged them to rebel against the Mughal rulers.

The Sikh Response

In reaction to the intensifying religious persecution during the Mughal era, Sikh leaders and gurus started promoting a more military strategy to safeguard their community and faith. In order to protect the Sikhs, the sixth Guru, Guru Hargobind Sahib, founded the Akal Sena, or army of the timeless one. The origins of the Sikh martial tradition were the result of this change. The Sikh community became increasingly determined to oppose political tyranny and religious oppression as a result of the Mughal war. An important turning point in Sikh history was the creation of the Khalsa under Guru Gobind Singh as a result of this spirit of resistance and the concerted efforts of Sikh leaders. An order of committed Sikhs dedicated to protecting their religion and society was known as the Khalsa. When the Khalsa was founded in 1699, its members had to abide by a stringent code of behavior, which included donning the five Ks, a sign of the Khalsa's dedication to Sikhism.

The ongoing conflicts between the Sikhs and the Mughals shaped the Sikh identity over time and resulted in the early 19th-century foundation of the Sikh Empire under Maharaja Ranjit Singh (1799–1839). Respect for religion and pluralism were hallmarks of the Sikh Empire, where Christians, Muslims, and Hindus held positions of authority. The Khalsa army was among the most powerful in South Asia, and the Sikh Empire was renowned for its military strength. The British East India Company seized the Sikh Empire in the middle of the 19th century, ending its existence.

An important turning point in Sikh history was the fighting against the Mughals, which shaped the Khalsa's formation and the evolution of Sikh identity. The Sikh community became more militarized as a result of the conflict, which was a major factor in the Sikh Empire's founding. The Sikh Empire had a long-lasting influence on South Asian history and was known for its military might and religious tolerance.

The Battle of Rohilla (1621)

The Battle of Hargobindpur, also recognized as the Battle of Rohilla, unfolded in the year 1621, standing as a pivotal campaign orchestrated by the Mughal Empire in response to the escalating influence of the Sikh community. The historical context leading up to this significant clash was marked by the persistent persecution of the Sikhs by the Mughal Empire, a persecution notably intensified by the martyrdom of Guru Arjan, the revered fifth Guru of the Sikhs and father of Guru Hargobind. This tragic event occurred on the orders of Jahangir, the Mughal emperor, setting the stage for a complex and consequential confrontation. The Battle of Hargobindpur, therefore, not only symbolized a military engagement but also embodied a crucial chapter in the ongoing struggle between the Sikhs and the Mughal Empire, shaping the dynamics of their historical relationship.

The Battle

Following the tragic martyrdom of Guru Arjan Dev, Guru Hargobind took decisive steps to militarize the Sikhs, transforming them into formidable militias with a focus on irregular cavalry warfare. This strategic shift resulted in an increase in both political and military power in the region, causing concern among local feudal lords, including Bhagwan Das Ghererh, who were relatives of Chandu Shah, a key figure in the persecution of Guru Arjan.

The immediate trigger for the Battle of Hargobindpur was the creation of the town of Hargobindpur, also known as Rohilla during that period. Tensions escalated when Bhagwan Das Ghererh, a descendant of Chandu Shah, was beheaded by the Sikhs. Bhagwan Das had spoken derogatory words about Guru Hargobind, and despite the Guru's advice to ignore them, the Sikhs found it intolerable. The sinful nature of Chandu's descendant fueled the conflict.

The Sikhs faced an attack from local jagirdars, including Rattan Chand and Karam Chand, who were sons of Chandu Shah, acting under the command of the Mughal Governor of Jalandhar, Abdul Khan. The assault took place as Guru Hargobind was engaged in rebuilding the town of Hargobindpur, left in ruins by the Mughals after Guru Arjan's martyrdom. In a resolute defense, the Sikhs repulsed the attacking forces, and the surviving Mughal Army segments fled the battlefield, resulting in a decisive victory for the Sikhs.

The aftermath of the initial skirmish saw a subsequent Mughal campaign aimed at countering the growing Sikh influence. A significant Mughal force, numbering between 4,000 and 15,000 troops, was dispatched to northern Punjab to support Governor Abdul Khan against Guru Hargobind. Despite heavy fighting and the loss of Sikh General Bhai Jattu northeast of Amritsar at Rohilla, the Sikhs ultimately prevailed. Ratan Chand, Karam Chand, and Abdul Khan were defeated and killed, including Abdul's sons, Nabi Bakhsh and Karim Bakhsh, significantly strengthening the Sikh cause. The Battle of Hargobindpur thus stands as a testament to the resilience and determination of the Sikh community in the face of adversity.

The Battle of Chamkaur (1704)

The Battle of Chamkaur, widely known as the Second Battle of Chamkaur Sahib, stands as a momentous event in Sikh history, portraying a significant clash between the Khalsa forces, led by Guru Gobind Singh, and a coalition comprising Mughal and Hindu hill chief forces under the command of Wazir Khan. This historical confrontation, eternally etched into the fabric of Sikh heritage, exemplified the resilience and valor of the Khalsa in the face of formidable adversaries. Guru Gobind Singh, the revered leader of the Sikh community, later made reference to this battle in his letter, Zafarnama, underscoring its enduring importance. The Battle of Chamkaur not only symbolized a crucial chapter in the Sikh struggle for religious freedom but also highlighted Guru Gobind Singh's unwavering dedication to the principles of justice, equality, and the defense of the Sikh faith.

The Beginning

Despite assurances of safe conduct, the Mughal soldiers, driven by the desire to claim Guru Gobind Singh's head as a trophy, besieged the haveli. The actual battle unfolded outside the haveli, where Guru Gobind Singh was resting. A council of Panj Piare (five beloved ones) was convened during the battle, urging the Guru to leave the battlefield to preserve his life and continue leading the Sikhs. In compliance with their request, Guru Gobind Singh escaped while negotiations broke down, and the Sikh soldiers bravely engaged the overwhelming Mughal forces. To confuse the enemy, another Sikh, Sangat Singh, donned the Guru's clothes and stayed with the soldiers. Tragically, the remaining Sikhs were killed by the Mughal forces the next morning.

In the aftermath, Guru Gobind Singh expressed pride in the sacrifice of his sons and proclaimed that he considered the Sikhs as his thousands of sons, known as the Singhs. He also voiced his distrust of Aurangzeb, the Mughal Emperor, due to the broken vow he took on the Quran.

The Zafarnama, or "Epistle of Victory," is a letter written by Guru Gobind Singh to Aurangzeb, vividly describing the events at Chamkaur. In the letter, Guru Gobind Singh holds Aurangzeb responsible for the broken promises and emphasizes the pride he takes in the bravery of his followers during the battle.

After escaping Chamkaur, the exhausted Guru was carried by two Pathans, Ghani Khan and Nabi Khan, to Jatpur, where he was received by a local Muslim chieftain. Subsequently, in Dina, he stayed at the house of Mayi Desan ji, where he composed the Zafarnama in Persian, consisting of 111 verses. The Zafarnama stands as a powerful testament to Guru Gobind Singh's resilience, courage, and commitment to justice in the face of adversity.

The Battle of Chappar Chiri (1710)

In the historic Battle of Chappar Chiri, also recognized as the Battle of Sirhind, the Mughal Empire and the Sikhs clashed on May 12, 1710, near Chappar Chiri, situated 20 kilometers from Sirhind. The Sikhs, driven by a quest for justice, embarked on a campaign against Sirhind, targeting its ruler Wazir Khan and dewan Sucha Nand. This mission sought retribution for the Mughal persecution and the tragic demise of Guru Gobind Singh's two young children in 1708. After the martyrdom of Guru Gobind Singh, the leadership of the Sikh army fell to Banda Singh Bahadur, who assumed the mantle to lead the Sikhs in their pursuit of justice and resistance against the Mughal oppression.

The Fight

The first Sikh military leader to launch an attack against India's Mughal emperors was Banda Singh Bahadur, better known by his nickname Baba Banda Singh Bahadur. He was a brilliant fighter. At first, Banda Singh Bahadur was a Bairagi Sadhu, going under the name Baba Madhav Das. He ruled for perhaps six or seven years, during a period when there was not a large Sikh community.

Because Baba Banda Singh Bahadur's army could supply the Mughal authority of Sirhind with military support, they conquered and pillaged several important cities along the route, including Sonepat, Kaithal, Samana, Shahabad, Mustafabad, and Sadhaura. Together with Baba Banda Singh and his soldiers, the number of looters increased, eager to take advantage of the wealth that lay outside Sirhind's walls. There, in the hamlet of Chappar Chiri, stood the armies of Wazir Khan and Baba Banda Singh.

Sher Muhammad Khan and Khwaja Ali of Malerkotla were among the several Mughal troops that were slain as Banda Singh himself charged into the conflict to support his army and decisively attack the imperial army. After Wazir Khan was also slain, his army was routed and forced to withdraw to Sirhind, where Banda's pursuit of them resulted in more deaths.

In his quest to establish Sikh supremacy in the area, Banda Singh Bahadur defeated the Mughals and their allies in a number of other wars including the Battle of Chappar Chiri. During his expedition leading up to Sirhind, some of the major fights were the seizure of Samana, Shahabad, Mustafabad, and Sadhaura. However, the Mughal rulers became aware of Banda Singh Bahadur's accomplishment and became enraged.

Six kilometers west of Gurdaspur, Punjab, near the village of Gurdas Nangal, the army commanded by Abd al-Samad Khan, the Mughal ruler of Lahore, forced Banda Bahadur and the Sikh forces into the village and besieged it in March 1715. After a grueling struggle at Gurdas Nangal, the Sikhs successfully maintained the little fort for eight months, but on December 7, 1715, Banda Singh Bahadur and his allies were defeated and taken prisoner by the Mughal army.

At that time, Banda Singh Bahadur was put to death brutally and inhumanely by the Mughal Emperor Farrukhsiyar in order to put an end to any future uprisings. Banda Singh Bahadur endured horrible abuse and torture, as did his close allies.

The surviving Sikhs were all shackled, and Banda Singh Bahadur was placed inside an iron cage. Along with the 780 Sikh prisoners, 2,000 Sikh heads put on spears, and 700 cartloads of murdered Sikh heads used to terrorize the populace, the Sikhs were carried to Delhi in a parade. They were imprisoned in the Delhi fort and subjected to coercion to convert to Islam.

These non-converters were told they would be put to death if they continued to refuse. 100 Sikh troops were taken out of the fort each day and executed in front of the populace. This went on like this for about seven days. He was instructed to murder Ajai Singh, his four-year-old son, but he refused. In other words, Ajai Singh was killed, his heart severed, and placed in Banda Bahadur's mouth. He was martyred because, while being tortured, his resolve did not waver. Following a three-month imprisonment, Banda Singh was executed on June 9, 1716, having his eyes gouged out, each of his limbs chopped, his skin taken, and his body burned.

The death of Banda Singh Bahadur, which represents the sacrifices made by Sikh leaders and followers in their pursuit of a just and equitable society, is a sad episode in the fight for religious freedom and justice. In the eyes of Sikh history, Banda Singh Bahadur remains a hero today, and his teachings encourage future generations to preserve the values of equality, fairness, and religious freedom.
